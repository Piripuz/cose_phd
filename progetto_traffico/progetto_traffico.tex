\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

As cities expand, becoming larger and larger
urban mobility is posing growing challenges to decision makers:
an increase of travel demand interferes indeed with a limited infrastructure,
causing a degeneration of travel condition, and several issues.

One of the biggest issues is congestion, which is pervasive in everyday's life,
and creates big inefficiencies in the system as a whole:
according to a recent study \parencite{kim2022congestion},
system inefficiencies leading to congestion
cause in California an average delay of four to five minutes per commuter per day,
amounting to an annual economic loss comparable to six billions USD,
equivalent to 1.67\% of the state's GDP.
These figures describe the effect of a systemic inefficiency,
to which a systemic answer needs to be found.

To address this issue, two main strategies can be adopted:
policies can be implemented to affect either the supply (that is, the infrastructure)
or the demand (that is, the behaviour of the users).
On the one hand, implementing policies on the supply side is the easiest way:
improving the infrastructure looks like an easy solution to a rise in congestion,
and is often adopted by policy makers.
Supply side solutions anyway draw many, different challenges.
Modifying the infrastructure may indeed be infeasible, for different kinds of constraints;
when feasible, it may be costly, or extremely slow.
On the other hand,
one may implement policies which aim to have an impact on the demand of the traffic,
known under the name of Traffic Demand Management (TDM).
TDM policies aim to influence when and how the road users move,
and with this influence to reduce road usage in peak hours and,
consequently, to reduce congestion.
A wide variety of these strategies exist:
examples may include policies such as implementation of fixed or variable tolls,
congestion pricing, High Occupancy Vehicle (HOV) lanes or parking restrictions,
as well as incentives for mode-switching like public transport or carpooling.

These policies are generally more flexible, and easier to implement,
than an infrastructural change.
Despite this, they require a deep understanding of how congestion is created
to effectively tackle it.
Many actors are indeed involved in how congestion develops:
the users, that choose how and when to travel;
governments, which make decisions regarding policy and infrastructure;
the service providers, who are in charge of implementing these policies and react to the users' choices.

Effectively implementing TDM strategies requires understanding how these agents interact and,
in particular, how the users choose when and how to travel.
This project proposal aims to deepen the knowledge of this topic.
The contribution of the project would be twofold:
on the theoretical side, I aim to improve the current models of congestion forming,
mainly dating back to \cite{d0907f84-e14a-3d98-ad20-759f41491d6e}.
The improvement will be targeted to solving existing issues in the model,
as noted in \cite{https://doi.org/10.1111/iere.12692}.
On the applied side, the model still needs to be properly validated with data,
and reliable values for its parameters need to be found via proper calibration of the model.
This calibration can be done by using several data sources,
including modern data sources such as drone imaging \parencite{fonod2025songdo}.

The project proposal is structured as follow:
section consists of a review of the existing literature,
describing how congestion theory was developed until now.
Section will explain the contributions of this project to the literature,
what I aim to improve and what methods will be used to achieve that aim.

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:lit}

Modelling congestion is not a new issue in the literature.
Early works, such as \cite{Jorgensen1947},
relied on static modelling:
the dependency of traffic on the time of the day was neglected,
typically considering different regimes for peak and off-peak congestion.

This approach was challenged by two seminal papers by William Vickrey (\cite{Vickrey1963, Vickrey1969}),
which gave the first example of what are now known as \textit{dynamic} traffic models,
laying the foundation of bottleneck modelling.
In these models, the traffic is not assumed to be constant on the time of the day.
More specifically, according to Vickrey's theory,
the road network has some junctions whose capacity is limited,
meaning that there exists a maximal flow of vehicles that can pass through them at any time.
These junctions are called \textit{bottleneck}, since they restrict the traffic flow as a bottleneck does with a flowing liquid.
Identifying the location and capacity of bottlenecks allows thus the forecasting of how traffic changes throughout the day,
depending on how people will choose to move.

Vickrey's work was later extended by \textcite{de1983stochastic},
which look for a stochastic equilibrium solution,
and \textcite{d0907f84-e14a-3d98-ad20-759f41491d6e},
which take into account elastic demand and better formalize the initial model.

These models better characterize how congestion is generated:
each user faces here a cost, depending on some user-dependant parameters,
decides when to commute based on the cost minimization.
In particular, the cost is assumed to be linear in travel time,
on the time the user arrives \textit{before} their target time
and on the time they arrive \textit{after} it, with three different coefficients:

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:cost_intro}
  C(t) = \alpha(\text{travel time}) + \beta (\text{time early}) + \gamma (\text{time late})
\end{equation}

Considering a cost as simple as this yields several advantages:
it indeed allows an equilibrium solution to be found \parencite{de1983stochastic},
as well as a complete characterization of the minima \parencite{yang2026recovering}.

Many works focused in finding an equilibrium solution when a group of users compete,
with different types of infrastructure:
originally, \textcite{de1983stochastic} found the solution for a group of homogenous users and a single bottleneck.
Here, each user independently minimizes their cost by shifting their departure time forward or backward.
The resulting equilibrium is achieved in a situation in which the travel time linearly increases on departure time,
until an on-time arrival is realized (that is, when \((\text{time early}) = (\text{time late}) = 0\)),
and linearly decreases after.
The steepness of increase and decrease depend uniquely on the coefficients \(\alpha, \beta, \gamma\).

Their work has been extended, taking into account more complex networks:
initially, \textcite{doi:10.1287/trsc.24.3.217} considers a Y-shaped network,
in which some of the users go through two different bottlenecks and some others only through one.
\textcite{doi:10.1287/trsc.27.2.148} consider again a Y-shaped network,
but with three distinct bottlenecks.
They find an equilibrium solution, and highlight how this situation yields a paradox:
by increasing the capacity of one link,
the total congestion at equilibrium becomes indeed higher.

More recently, \textcite{AKAMATSU2015808} analytically studied a network composed by a corridor along which several bottlenecks are positioned.
They develop a method that allows to analytically find an equilibrium solution for bigger networks,
with an arbitrary number of bottlenecks.

Similarly to how increasingly complex networks have been studied,
recent literature has been increasing the complexity of the population of users.

Initial works, such as \textcite{Vickrey1969}, considered the users to be homogeneous.
Later works relaxed this hypothesis, taking into account users with heterogeneous preferences or different desired arrival times.
Notably, \textcite{arnott1988schedule} analytically solves the problem to equilibrium when the users are divided in a finite number of groups,
each one of which has different values of \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\),
but constant ratio \(\gamma/\beta\).
They show that the slope of the travel time function gradually increases when getting closer to the desired arrival time,
and the groups of users with higher penalties for arriving early and late will arrive when the slopes are higher.

More recently, \textcite{amirgholy2017analytical} compute an analytical solution in the case of uniformly distributed preferences,
and numerically approximate it in case of other distributions.

For a deeper review of bottleneck modelling, please refer to \textcite{LI2020311};
The proposal will now focus more on the literature relevant for the project and,
in particular, on the problems encountered when validating the model with real data.

\textcite{54d203ee-4bf8-3234-9286-56e4c8b7f5bd} does a first attempt in doing so:
it indeed tries to find values for the ratios \(\beta/\alpha, \gamma/\alpha\),
relative to the parameters in equation \eqref{eq:cost_intro}.
It does so by fitting a discrete choice model on survey data,
after conducting a survey in which 527 commuters in the USA stated when they arrive to work.
The values found and reported by \textcite{54d203ee-4bf8-3234-9286-56e4c8b7f5bd} are widely used in the literature:
as in, for instance, \textcite{d0907f84-e14a-3d98-ad20-759f41491d6e}.
\textcite{https://doi.org/10.1111/iere.12692} presents a review of the parameter values used in the literature.

Furthermore, \textcite{https://doi.org/10.1111/iere.12692} highlights a relevant issue in calibrating the model with data and,
as a consequence, in the whole theory:
when studying data about real travel time patterns (that is, how the travel time across a real bottleneck change through the day),
observed slopes significantly differ from the theoretically predicted ones.

The author of the article suggests a solution, that is,
to consider a big amount of the travellers as \textit{inframarginal},
travellers who are not willing to change their departure time.
While this solution seemingly solves the issue,
it presents several issues, that will be addressed in the next section.

After this discrepancy with real data is solved,
the model needs to be properly calibrated with real data.
Numerical simulators of road traffic,
such as METROPOLIS and METROPOLIS2 \parencite{de1997metropolis,javaudin2024metropolis2},
already converge to a satisfactory approximation of the theoretical equilibria
\parencite{javaudin2024metropolis2},
and can efficiently be employed as a bridge between the theory and its application in policy making.
To converge to meaningful solutions,
they need anyway to rely on a proper description of the population.

The next section is devoted to describing how the project could tackle these issues,
and what results it could provide.
